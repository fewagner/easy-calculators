How the physics is modelled

The silicon lens is standard geometric ray optics — no wave/interference effects. At every silicon surface (the ellipsoidal dome and the flat) the tracer applies:

  • Snell refraction using the silicon index nSi = √εr ≈ 3.42, with the refracted direction d⃗t = η d⃗ + (η cosθi − cosθt) n⃗, η = n1/n2;
  • total internal reflection when sin2θt ≥ 1;
  • Fresnel reflection ½(Rs + Rp) deciding the reflected vs transmitted split — or, with the AR coating on (default), a fixed 3 % residual.

Metals (dish, lid, cavity walls) are specular mirrors that lose 1 − Rmat of the ray's intensity per bounce. The chip plane at the focus carries the central qubit absorber (radius Rant, red; rays that land inside it terminate) surrounded by a porous ground plane that reflects a fraction Rgp and transmits the rest into the cavity. The line opacity tracks the remaining intensity, so you watch energy bleed into the walls at each lossy bounce.

Where the frequency enters — only two places, both lumped:

  1. Diffraction blur. Each ray leaves the dish with a small Gaussian angular spread σθ = 0.437 λ/Rcurv (the dish's λ/D diffraction limit). Lower frequency → wider cone → softer focus. This is the only wave-optics effect, collapsed into a launch-angle spread. (The on-screen “diffraction fan” slider only exaggerates this so the cone is visible; it does not change the model.)
  2. Absorber size. The matched-slot qubit radius Rant = λ/(2π√εeff) shrinks with frequency — a smaller target at high bands.

Everything else is frequency-independent: the silicon index (silicon is non-dispersive at THz), the AR residual, and the wall reflectivity are all constants. So the lens itself is pure ray optics; frequency only sets how tightly the beam focuses and how big a target the qubit presents.

Why placement and wall finish matter (what the sweeps showed). The cavity is sealed, so essentially every photon ends either in the qubit or absorbed by the metal — collection is a race between qubit-absorption and metal-loss, decided by how many lossy bounces a photon makes before it finds the (small) absorber. That is why the detector must sit at the focal point (drag θ and watch the recycled rays re-converge there) and why wall reflectivity Rmat is the dominant lever — lower it and the rays fade out into the walls before reaching the qubit.
