2. Derivation of the mutual capacitance

2.1 Strategy. No closed-form exact solution exists for the mutual capacitance between a circular island and a CPW strip with surrounding ground plane. The formula below is a geometric view-factor approximation that integrates an angle-dependent coupling kernel over the effective feedline width. It captures all correct parametric trends and is accurate to a factor of 2–3 versus full EM simulation.

2.2 Effective parameters. Charge in a CPW structure is not concentrated only at the conductor edge — it distributes across the gap. Each conductor+gap pair is represented by an effective size placed at the midpoint between the conductor edge and the midpoint of the gap:

\[R_\text{eff} = \frac{d}{2} + \frac{s}{4}, \qquad W_\text{eff} = \frac{w}{2} + \frac{a}{4}\]

For the island: the conductor edge is at \(d/2\), the gap midpoint is at \(d/2 + s/2\), so the midpoint between them is \(d/2 + s/4\). The same logic gives \(w/2 + a/4\) for the feedline. This is not the gap midpoint \(d/2 + s/2\) — charge concentrates toward the conductor edge, so the effective radius is shifted inward.

Why the quarter-point? There is no unique derivation that pins down the prefactor to exactly \(\tfrac{1}{4}\) — it is an approximation for where the "effective centre of charge" of the CPW gap region sits. The physical argument is:

  • The surface charge density on a CPW conductor peaks at the inner edge (at \(d/2\)) — it diverges weakly there due to the sharp corner, following a well-known \((x-d/2)^{-1/2}\) singularity from conformal mapping.
  • The charge density is zero in the gap itself (no conductor, no free charge).
  • The ground plane at \(d/2 + s\) carries equal and opposite charge; its contribution cancels in the monopole far field, leaving a dipole of moment \(\propto Q\cdot s\).

The centroid of the gap-field distribution therefore lies closer to the inner conductor edge than to the gap midpoint. The \(s/4\) prescription places the effective radius at the midpoint between the conductor edge and the gap centre — i.e., halfway between \(d/2\) and \(d/2+s/2\). This is consistent with a first-order moment of the CPW charge distribution near the edge. In a full 2D conformal-mapping analysis of a CPW, the charge distribution in the gap region is non-uniform and skewed toward the inner edge, and the true centroid is indeed between \(d/2\) and \(d/2+s/2\). The factor \(\tfrac{1}{4}\) is a physically motivated estimate; the exact value is geometry-dependent and is one of the sources of the ~2–3× overall uncertainty of the formula.

2.3 View-factor integral. Consider the transverse distance \(x\) from the island centre to a strip of the feedline, measured perpendicular to the feedline axis at the closest-approach cross-section (\(y = 0\)). The half-angle subtended by the island (radius \(R_\text{eff}\)) from that transverse distance is \(\theta(x) = \arcsin(R_\text{eff}/x)\) — the angle you would need to look away from the transverse axis to just graze the island boundary, when standing at transverse distance \(x\) directly across from the island centre. The island spatial extent enters here: a larger \(R_\text{eff}\) means a larger \(\theta\) at every \(x\), hence more coupling everywhere across the feedline width. The coupling kernel \(\sec\theta - 1 = x/\sqrt{x^2 - R_\text{eff}^2} - 1\) vanishes as \(x\to\infty\) and diverges as \(x\to R_\text{eff}\) (contact). Integrating across the effective feedline width \([D - W_\text{eff},\; D + W_\text{eff}]\):

\[C_\text{mf} = 4\,\varepsilon_\text{eff}\,\varepsilon_0 \int_{D-W_\text{eff}}^{D+W_\text{eff}} \!\!\left[\frac{x}{\sqrt{x^2 - R_\text{eff}^2}} - 1\right] dx\]

The integration variable \(x\) runs across the effective feedline width \([D-W_\text{eff},\, D+W_\text{eff}]\) — the transverse direction, perpendicular to the feedline axis. The formula evaluates the geometry at the closest-approach cross-section (\(y = 0\)) only; the feedline's longitudinal extent is not integrated. This is a 1D cross-sectional approximation. The omitted longitudinal contribution carries the same \(1/D^2\) distance dependence as the formula and differs only by an \(O(1)\) prefactor, which is absorbed into the overall ±factor-of-2–3 accuracy.

2.4 Closed-form result. Using the antiderivative \(\int\!\left[x/\sqrt{x^2-R^2}-1\right]dx = \sqrt{x^2-R^2}-x+\text{const}\,\):

\[C_\text{mf} \approx 4\,\varepsilon_\text{eff}\,\varepsilon_0 \Bigl[\sqrt{(D+W_\text{eff})^2 - R_\text{eff}^2} - \sqrt{(D-W_\text{eff})^2 - R_\text{eff}^2} - 2\,W_\text{eff}\Bigr]\]

Note on the prefactor \(4\varepsilon_\text{eff}\varepsilon_0\). This prefactor is not derived from first principles — it is chosen so that the large-\(D\) limit matches the expected dipole scaling. A full 2D calculation (integrating over both transverse and longitudinal directions) yields a prefactor of \(2\varepsilon_\text{eff}\varepsilon_0\) at large \(D\):

\[C_\text{mf}^\text{full 2D} \;\longrightarrow\; \frac{2\,\varepsilon_\text{eff}\,\varepsilon_0\,R_\text{eff}^2\,W_\text{eff}}{D^2}\]

The factor-of-2 difference is a direct and concrete consequence of the 1D cross-sectional approximation described above — the formula overcounts by a factor of ~2 because it evaluates the longitudinal geometry only at \(y=0\) rather than integrating over all \(y\). This is one of the identifiable sources of the stated ±factor-of-2–3 accuracy.

Validity condition (conductors do not overlap in effective-parameter space):

\[D - W_\text{eff} > R_\text{eff} \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad D > \frac{d}{2} + \frac{s}{4} + \frac{w}{2} + \frac{a}{4}\]

Accuracy regime: the formula is most reliable when \(D/R_\text{eff} > 5\). At \(D/R_\text{eff} \approx 5\) (the nominal geometry with \(R_\text{eff} \approx 65\,\mu\text{m}\)), accuracy is ~factor-of-2–3. At \(D/R_\text{eff} < 3\), the cross-sectional approximation breaks down more severely and EM simulation (Sonnet, HFSS, Palace) is the only reliable option.

2.5 Large-distance limit. Expanding for \(D \gg W_\text{eff}, R_\text{eff}\):

\[C_\text{mf} \;\longrightarrow\; \frac{4\,\varepsilon_\text{eff}\,\varepsilon_0\,R_\text{eff}^2\,W_\text{eff}}{D^2} \qquad(1/D^2 \text{ dipole-like decay})\]

At the nominal geometry \(D/R_\text{eff} \approx 5\), the full formula is needed — the island body contributes significantly and cannot be treated as a point dipole.

3. From C_mf to T₁

Modelling the qubit as an LC oscillator with total capacitance \(C_\Sigma\), the admittance seen through \(C_\text{mf}\) into a matched feedline (impedance \(Z_0\)) is \(Y(\omega) = j\omega C_\text{mf}/(1 + j\omega C_\text{mf}Z_0)\). Since \(\omega C_\text{mf}Z_0 \sim 10^{-4} \ll 1\):

\[\operatorname{Re}[Y(\omega)] \approx \omega^2 C_\text{mf}^2\,Z_0\]

An infinite feedline has two ports — the factor of 2 is essential and often omitted in the literature:

\[\kappa = \frac{2\,\omega^2 C_\text{mf}^2\,Z_0}{C_\Sigma}, \qquad T_1 = \frac{C_\Sigma}{2\,\omega^2 C_\text{mf}^2\,Z_0}\]

This follows from input-output theory (Blais et al., Rev. Mod. Phys. 93, 025005, 2021; Koch et al., Phys. Rev. A 76, 042319, 2007). \(C_\text{mf}\) is the mutual capacitance above; \(C_\Sigma\) is the total qubit self-capacitance (typically 40–100 fF for a 125 µm transmon island).
